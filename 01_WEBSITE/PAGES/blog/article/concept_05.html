Why our deficiency walks happen before invoicing.

A short, opinionated piece on the most underrated step in handover.

The deficiency walk is the part of the process nobody likes. By the time you reach it, the build is "done" — the client wants to occupy, the team wants to demobilize, the punch list feels like a chore. So it gets pushed.

The conventional schedule looks like this: deficiency walk, invoice issued, punch list resolved over the following month. We do it the other way: deficiency walk, punch list closed to zero, then the invoice issues. Different sequence, same components.

The math of that swap.

When the invoice issues with deficiencies open, the client now has a list, an invoice, and an empty space. Two of those things create urgency; the third doesn't. The list waits. Two weeks later, the client emails. Three weeks later, the trades have rebooked. Four weeks later, the project is "almost done" — a phrase that signals trouble.

What changes when the order flips.

Trades stay on hook because they don't get paid until the list is closed. We stay on hook because we hold our own invoice. The client gets a finished space, not an almost-finished one. The PM gets to start the next job from a clean line.

It's not a clever trick. It's just the only sequence that respects the word "complete."

The hard part.

The hard part is scoping it correctly at the front. Deficiency walks done before invoicing means the schedule includes the closing-out time, not just the install time. We add two weeks. Sometimes three. We tell the client in week one that the final two weeks of the schedule are punch-resolution. They like it. The schedule is honest from the start.
